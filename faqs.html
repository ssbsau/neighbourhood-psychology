Starting your journey

I've never spoken with anyone about what I'm going through. What can I expect?

It's great that you're taking this step. Seeing someone for the first time can feel a little nerve-wracking, but knowing what to expect can make it easier.

In your first appointment:

  • Introductions & getting comfortable — your psychologist will explain how the process works, including confidentiality and what you can expect from sessions.
  • Talking about why you're there — you don't need to have all the answers. They may ask open-ended questions like "What brought you in today?" or "How have you been feeling lately?"
  • Background — they might ask about your mental health history, any significant life events, relationships, work or school stress, sleep and physical health.
  • Setting goals — if you're comfortable, you can discuss what you hope to get from therapy. If you're not sure, that's okay — they can help you figure it out.
  • Next steps — your psychologist may suggest a plan for future sessions or coping strategies to try in the meantime.
What if I'm not comfortable with my psychologist?

That's totally normal. Finding the right fit can take a little trial and error. If you don't feel comfortable after a couple of sessions, it's okay to try someone else. There will be no pressure.

Is everything we talk about confidential?

Yes. Everything we talk about is confidential. The only exceptions are rare situations where we are legally required to act to keep you or someone else safe — and we would always discuss this with you where possible.

Telehealth

Does online therapy really work?

Yes — research consistently shows that online psychology sessions are just as effective as face-to-face appointments for most concerns. Telehealth can be a very effective way of helping most people, and many clients find it more convenient and just as comfortable as in-person sessions.

Are online sessions private and confidential?

Yes. Our online sessions are private and confidential. We use secure, encrypted telehealth platforms, and everything we discuss is handled with the same professional and legal confidentiality standards as in-person appointments.

Is Telehealth available 24/7?

Telehealth is generally only available during our business hours. In some cases, sessions outside these hours may be arranged by prior agreement.

If you need support outside our operating hours, please contact Lifeline on 13 11 14 or call 000 in an emergency.

Support for children

How do we know if our child needs to see a psychologist?

If your child is experiencing ongoing changes in behaviour, mood, sleep, school engagement, or emotional regulation, support from a psychologist can help. We work with families to understand what's happening and whether therapy is the right next step.

Will parents be involved in our child's therapy?

Yes. We take a collaborative approach and involve parents where appropriate. This may include check-ins, guidance strategies and support at home, while still respecting your child's privacy and developmental needs.

How do you help children feel comfortable in sessions?

We create a warm, safe and age-appropriate environment where children can feel at ease. Sessions are tailored to your child's age and needs and may include play-based, creative or practical strategies to help them express themselves and build skills.

Support for adolescents

What issues can adolescents get support with?

We support adolescents with a range of concerns, including anxiety, low mood, stress, emotional regulation, self-esteem, school pressures, social challenges and family changes.

How is confidentiality handled for teenagers?

Adolescents are entitled to confidentiality in therapy. We explain this clearly to both teens and parents at the outset, with the understanding that parents will be involved if there are safety concerns or serious risks.

How do you engage teenagers who may be reluctant to attend?

We take a respectful, collaborative approach and meet adolescents where they are. Building trust is a priority, and sessions are tailored to be practical, relevant and engaging — helping young people feel heard rather than judged.

Support for adults & older adults

What concerns can adults and older adults seek support for?

We support adults and older adults with concerns such as anxiety, depression, stress, grief and loss, trauma, chronic health adjustment, life transitions, retirement, and relationship or family changes.

What can I expect from the first session?

The first session is focused on understanding what's been happening, your goals and how we can best support you. It's also an opportunity to ask questions and ensure the approach feels comfortable and appropriate for your stage of life.

Do I need a referral to see a psychologist?

No referral is required to book an appointment. However, if you wish to access Medicare rebates, a Mental Health Care Plan from your GP is needed.

Support for families

How can family therapy help us?

If your family is dealing with ongoing conflict, big changes, stress or communication breakdowns that feel hard to resolve on your own, family support can help everyone understand each other better and figure out practical ways forward. It's useful even when the issue seems small — early support can stop things from getting bigger.

Do all family members need to attend every session?

Not always. Sessions are tailored to your family's needs and may involve different combinations of family members at different times, depending on the goals of therapy.

What if our family has a mix of children, teenagers and adults?

That's very common. We adapt our approach to suit each family member's age and role, helping everyone feel heard while working together toward shared goals.

More information: Child & Family Therapy

Support for active and retired military personnel

What types of support are available for current and former military members?

We support active and retired military personnel with concerns including transition to civilian life, trauma, stress, anxiety, mood difficulties, relationship challenges and adjustment to life after service.

Do I need to have experienced combat to seek support?

No. Support is available for a wide range of service-related and personal challenges. Whether difficulties stem from operational duties, workplace stress, family impacts or life transitions, therapy can be helpful.

Will the psychologist understand military culture?

Yes. Dean served in the Air Force for 13 years, including deployment. We recognise the unique demands, values and experiences of military life. Our approach is respectful, practical and informed by an understanding of operational culture, while remaining focused on your individual needs.
